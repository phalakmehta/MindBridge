# Behavioral Activation

## Overview
Behavioral activation is an evidence-based CBT technique primarily used for depression. It is based on the principle that depression leads to withdrawal and inactivity, which in turn worsens depression. By scheduling positive, meaningful, or mastery-based activities, the cycle of avoidance is broken.

## When to Use
- When a person reports feeling unmotivated, withdrawn, or "stuck"
- When depression is causing reduced activity levels
- When someone says "I don't feel like doing anything"
- When avoidance behavior is maintaining low mood

## Core Concept
Depression creates a vicious cycle:
Low mood → Reduced activity → Less positive reinforcement → Lower mood

Behavioral activation reverses this:
Scheduled activity → Some positive reinforcement → Slightly improved mood → More motivation for activity

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Activity Monitoring
Ask the person to track their daily activities and rate each one for:
- Pleasure (0-10): How enjoyable was it?
- Mastery (0-10): How much of a sense of accomplishment did it give?

Example prompt: "Over the next few days, could you note down what you do each hour and how it makes you feel? This will help us understand your current patterns."

### Step 2: Identify Values and Goals
Explore what matters to the person — relationships, career, health, creativity, spirituality. Activities should be aligned with personal values.

Example prompt: "When you think about what really matters to you in life, what comes to mind?"

### Step 3: Create an Activity Menu
Together, brainstorm a list of activities across categories:
- **Social**: Calling a friend, having coffee with someone
- **Physical**: Walking, stretching, dancing
- **Productive**: Cleaning one drawer, replying to one email
- **Pleasurable**: Listening to music, cooking a favorite meal
- **Mastery**: Learning something new, completing a small task

### Step 4: Schedule Activities
Start small. Schedule 1-2 activities per day. Use specific times and keep the bar very low.

Example prompt: "Shall we pick one small activity you could try tomorrow? It doesn't have to be big — even a 10-minute walk counts."

### Step 5: Review and Adjust
After completing activities, review what the person noticed about their mood before, during, and after. Celebrate any effort, regardless of outcome.

## Key Principles
- Start extremely small — the goal is action, not perfection
- "Action before motivation" — don't wait to feel like doing something
- Focus on what the person CAN do, not what they can't
- Validate that doing things when depressed is genuinely hard
- Progress is measured by effort, not by mood change (mood change follows later)

## Important Notes
- This technique is one of the most evidence-supported interventions for mild to moderate depression
- It can be combined with cognitive restructuring for greater effect
- Avoid overwhelming the person with too many activities at once
- Always acknowledge the effort it takes to be active when feeling low
