# Exposure Hierarchy

## Overview
Exposure therapy is a CBT technique used to help individuals gradually confront feared situations, objects, or internal sensations. By creating a hierarchy of feared situations ranked from least to most anxiety-provoking, individuals can systematically work through their fears in a controlled, manageable way.

## When to Use
- Specific phobias (heights, spiders, flying, etc.)
- Social anxiety
- Panic disorder with agoraphobia
- Obsessive-compulsive patterns
- Post-traumatic stress
- Generalized anxiety with avoidance behaviors

## Core Concepts

### Habituation
When a person stays in contact with a feared stimulus without escaping, their anxiety naturally decreases over time. The brain learns that the feared outcome doesn't occur or is manageable.

### Safety Behaviors
These are subtle avoidance strategies people use to feel safe during exposure (e.g., holding onto someone, carrying medication "just in case"). These must be gradually eliminated for full benefit.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Psychoeducation
Explain how avoidance maintains fear. Use the metaphor of the "anxiety wave" — anxiety rises, peaks, and naturally comes down if you stay with it.

Example prompt: "Avoidance feels helpful in the moment, but it actually teaches your brain that the situation really is dangerous. Each time you avoid, the fear grows a little stronger."

### Step 2: Build the Hierarchy
List 8-15 situations related to the fear, from least to most anxiety-provoking. Rate each with a SUDS score (Subjective Units of Distress Scale, 0-100).

Example hierarchy for social anxiety:
- Saying hello to a cashier (SUDS: 15)
- Making a phone call to schedule an appointment (SUDS: 30)
- Eating alone in a café (SUDS: 40)
- Starting a conversation with an acquaintance (SUDS: 55)
- Attending a small group gathering (SUDS: 65)
- Giving a short presentation to a few people (SUDS: 75)
- Speaking up in a large meeting (SUDS: 85)

### Step 3: Begin with the Lowest Item
Start with the situation that causes the least anxiety. The person should stay in the situation until their anxiety drops by at least 50% or to a manageable level.

### Step 4: Repeat Until Comfortable
Repeat the same exposure until it no longer causes significant anxiety (SUDS below 20-25). Then move to the next item.

### Step 5: Progress Up the Hierarchy
Work through each item sequentially. Some items may need to be broken into smaller steps.

## Key Principles
- Never force someone into an exposure they haven't agreed to
- Go at the person's pace — this is collaborative, not coercive
- Celebrate every attempt, regardless of how it went
- Setbacks are normal and expected — they're part of the process
- The goal is not to eliminate anxiety, but to learn it's manageable

## Important Safety Notes
- Exposure should NEVER be used for situations that are genuinely dangerous
- For trauma-related exposures, this should be guided by a trained professional
- If a person becomes extremely distressed, it's okay to pause and use grounding techniques
- Imaginal exposure (visualizing the feared scenario) can be used when in-vivo exposure isn't possible
