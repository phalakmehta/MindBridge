# Problem Solving Therapy

## Overview
Problem-solving therapy is a structured CBT approach that helps individuals develop effective strategies for coping with life's challenges. It teaches a systematic method for addressing problems rather than feeling overwhelmed by them. The approach is particularly useful when someone feels stuck or believes their problems are unsolvable.

## When to Use
- When a person feels overwhelmed by multiple problems
- When someone tends to avoid dealing with issues
- When emotional distress is linked to specific, identifiable problems
- When decision-making feels paralyzing
- When someone keeps ruminating about what to do

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Problem Identification and Definition
Help the person clearly define the problem. Vague problems are unsolvable; specific ones are manageable.

Unhelpful: "My life is a mess."
Helpful: "I'm struggling to manage my workload because I have three deadlines this week and I haven't started any of them."

Example prompt: "Let's try to get specific. When you say things feel overwhelming, what's the main thing that's weighing on you right now?"

### Step 2: Set a Realistic Goal
What would a good outcome look like? The goal should be specific, achievable, and within the person's control.

Example prompt: "If this problem were resolved, what would that look like? What's a realistic outcome you'd be happy with?"

### Step 3: Brainstorm Solutions
Generate as many solutions as possible WITHOUT judging them. Quantity over quality at this stage. Even silly or extreme ideas are welcome — they often spark more practical ones.

Example prompt: "Let's come up with every possible way you could handle this — good ideas, bad ideas, creative ideas, even ones that seem unrealistic. We'll evaluate them afterward."

### Step 4: Evaluate Solutions
For each solution, consider:
- Pros and cons
- Short-term vs. long-term consequences
- Feasibility (time, resources, energy)
- Alignment with values

### Step 5: Choose and Plan
Select the best solution (or combination). Create a specific action plan:
- What exactly will you do?
- When will you do it?
- What resources do you need?
- What obstacles might arise, and how will you handle them?

### Step 6: Implement and Review
After implementing the plan, review what happened. Did it work? What would you do differently? What did you learn?

## Key Principles
- Problems feel bigger when they're vague — defining them is half the solution
- There's rarely one "perfect" solution — good enough is good enough
- Breaking large problems into smaller parts makes them manageable
- Emotional readiness matters — if someone is too distressed, use calming techniques first
- Celebrate the process of problem-solving, not just the outcome
- Not all problems can be solved — accepting this is sometimes the most therapeutic step

## Problem Orientation
Before problem-solving, check the person's orientation toward problems:
- **Negative orientation**: "Problems shouldn't happen to me," "I can't handle this," "Nothing will work"
- **Positive orientation**: "Problems are a normal part of life," "I can learn to cope," "I've handled difficult things before"

If the orientation is negative, gently challenge it before moving into the structured process.
