# Behavioral Experiments

## Overview
Behavioral experiments are structured activities designed to test the validity of a person's beliefs through real-world experience. Instead of simply discussing whether a thought is accurate, the person actively tests it. This makes the learning experiential and often more powerful than purely verbal cognitive restructuring.

## When to Use
- When cognitive restructuring alone isn't shifting a belief
- When someone intellectually understands a thought is distorted but "still feels true"
- When avoidance is maintained by specific predictions ("If I do X, then Y will happen")
- When someone is ready to take a small risk to test their beliefs
- When a belief can be tested safely in the real world

## Types of Behavioral Experiments

### 1. Hypothesis Testing
The person makes a specific prediction, carries out a behavior, and observes the actual outcome.

Example:
- Belief: "If I speak up in the meeting, everyone will think I'm stupid."
- Experiment: Speak up once in the next meeting.
- Prediction: People will laugh, roll their eyes, or dismiss me.
- Actual Outcome: (Recorded after the experiment)

### 2. Discovery Experiments
When the person isn't sure what will happen, they try something new just to gather information.

Example:
- Belief: "I can't cope with social situations."
- Experiment: Attend a casual social event for 30 minutes.
- Question: "What do I notice about my experience?"

### 3. Survey Experiments
The person tests their belief by asking others for their perspective.

Example:
- Belief: "Everyone thinks it's weird to go to a restaurant alone."
- Experiment: Ask 5 people if they've ever eaten alone and how they felt about it.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify the Target Belief
Write down the specific belief to test. Make it as concrete and testable as possible.

Not testable: "I'm worthless"
Testable: "If I ask for help at work, my boss will see me as incompetent"

### Step 2: Rate Belief Strength
How strongly does the person believe this (0-100%)?

### Step 3: Design the Experiment
Plan what they'll do, when, and where. Anticipate obstacles and plan for them.

Questions to consider:
- "What exactly will you do?"
- "When and where will you try this?"
- "What might get in the way? How will you handle that?"
- "How will you know what the outcome was?"

### Step 4: Make Predictions
Before the experiment, record specific predictions:
- "What do you think will happen?"
- "How anxious do you think you'll feel (0-100)?"
- "How do you think others will react?"

### Step 5: Carry Out the Experiment
Do it. The person should try to observe what happens rather than focusing on their anxiety.

### Step 6: Record the Outcome
After the experiment, write down exactly what happened — not what it "felt like" happened, but the observable facts.

### Step 7: Reflect
- "What actually happened vs. what you predicted?"
- "What did you learn?"
- "How strong is the original belief now (0-100%)?"
- "What does this tell you about the belief?"

## Example Experiment Worksheet

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Belief | "If I say no to a friend's request, they'll be angry and stop speaking to me" |
| Belief strength before | 80% |
| Experiment | Politely decline the next request I'm not comfortable with |
| Prediction | They will be visibly upset, make a sarcastic comment, and not call me for weeks |
| Actual Outcome | They said "No problem, maybe next time" and texted me the next day about something else |
| Belief strength after | 35% |
| What I learned | My friend respected my boundary. Saying no didn't ruin the friendship. |

## Key Principles
- Start with lower-stakes experiments to build confidence
- The experiment must be genuinely collaborative — never pressure someone
- Regardless of the outcome, there's learning — "failed" experiments provide valuable data
- Sometimes the feared outcome DOES happen, and that's okay — the learning is about coping capacity
- Record everything — memory is unreliable, especially when anxious
- Build on success: each experiment makes the next one easier
- These experiments often produce more lasting change than verbal techniques alone

## Safety Notes
- Never design an experiment that puts the person in genuine danger
- Start with the least anxiety-provoking test possible
- Have a coping plan ready in case the experiment triggers significant distress
- If the person isn't ready, that's okay — readiness is part of the process
