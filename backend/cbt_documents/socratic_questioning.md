# Socratic Questioning

## Overview
Socratic questioning is a method of guided discovery where the counsellor asks carefully crafted questions to help the person examine their own thoughts, beliefs, and assumptions. Rather than telling someone what to think, the counsellor guides them to discover insights on their own. This approach respects the person's autonomy and leads to deeper, more lasting change.

## When to Use
- When a person holds a belief they haven't examined
- When someone presents a thought as absolute fact
- When you want them to discover alternative perspectives on their own
- When direct challenge might feel confrontational or dismissive
- Throughout cognitive restructuring as the primary questioning method

## Categories of Socratic Questions

### 1. Clarifying Questions
Help the person articulate their thought more precisely.
- "What do you mean when you say...?"
- "Can you give me an example of that?"
- "When you say 'always,' can you think of any exceptions?"
- "What specifically about the situation bothers you most?"

### 2. Probing Assumptions
Reveal underlying beliefs and assumptions.
- "What are you assuming here?"
- "Why do you think that is true?"
- "What if the opposite were true?"
- "Where did this belief come from?"
- "Is this something you've always believed, or is it newer?"

### 3. Evidence-Based Questions
Encourage examination of support for beliefs.
- "What evidence supports this thought?"
- "What evidence goes against it?"
- "How do you know this is true?"
- "If a friend told you this, what would you say to them?"
- "Has there been a time when this thought turned out to be inaccurate?"

### 4. Perspective Questions
Help see the situation from different angles.
- "How might someone else see this situation?"
- "What would you tell your best friend if they were in this situation?"
- "How will you feel about this in a week? A year?"
- "What would a compassionate observer say about this?"

### 5. Consequence Questions
Explore the implications of the belief.
- "What happens when you believe this thought?"
- "How does this belief affect your behavior?"
- "What's the cost of continuing to think this way?"
- "What might change if you held this belief less tightly?"

### 6. Meta-Questions
Questions about the thinking process itself.
- "What does it say about you that you're able to question this thought?"
- "Is this a pattern you've noticed before?"
- "What kind of thinking is this? Does it have a name?"

## Examples in Practice

### Scenario: "Nobody likes me"
1. "When you say 'nobody,' do you mean literally every person?" (Clarifying)
2. "What makes you believe no one likes you?" (Evidence)
3. "Can you think of anyone who has shown they care about you?" (Counter-evidence)
4. "If your friend said 'nobody likes me,' what would you tell them?" (Perspective)
5. "How does believing this affect how you interact with people?" (Consequence)

### Scenario: "I should be able to handle this on my own"
1. "Where does that expectation come from?" (Assumption)
2. "Do you hold other people to the same standard?" (Perspective)
3. "What counts as 'handling it' in your mind?" (Clarifying)
4. "What are the consequences of insisting on handling everything alone?" (Consequence)

## Key Principles
- The therapist genuinely doesn't know what the person will discover — stay curious
- Pace matters: one good question is better than a rapid-fire series
- Allow silence after asking a question — let the person think
- Follow their answers with more questions rather than offering your own conclusions
- If a question lands and creates a moment of insight, sit with it — don't rush
- Avoid questions that are actually statements in disguise ("Don't you think you should...?")
- The tone should be curious and warm, never interrogative
