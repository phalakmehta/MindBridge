# Thought Records

## Overview
A thought record is a structured CBT worksheet that helps individuals systematically examine their thoughts, emotions, and behaviors in response to specific situations. It is one of the most widely used CBT tools for building self-awareness and developing the skill of cognitive restructuring.

## When to Use
- When a person experiences recurring negative thoughts
- When someone wants a structured way to process difficult emotions
- When developing the habit of examining thoughts between sessions
- As homework between conversations to deepen self-understanding

## The 7-Column Thought Record

### Column 1: Date and Time
When did the situation occur?

### Column 2: Situation
What happened? Describe objectively — who, what, where, when. Keep it factual.
Example: "My manager didn't say hello to me this morning when I walked in."

### Column 3: Automatic Thoughts
What thoughts went through your mind? Write them down exactly as they occurred, even if they seem irrational.
Example: "She's angry at me. I must have done something wrong. I'm going to get fired."

### Column 4: Emotions
What emotions did you feel? Rate each from 0-100%.
Example: "Anxiety (80%), Sadness (60%), Fear (70%)"

### Column 5: Evidence For the Thought
What facts support the automatic thought?
Example: "She usually says good morning. She seemed busy and distracted."

### Column 6: Evidence Against the Thought
What facts contradict the automatic thought?
Example: "She was on the phone when I walked in. She's been stressed about a deadline. She smiled at me later in the meeting. She's never given me negative feedback."

### Column 7: Balanced Thought & Re-rated Emotions
What is a more balanced perspective? How do you feel now?
Example: "She was probably preoccupied with work. It likely had nothing to do with me. Anxiety (30%), Sadness (20%)"

## How to Introduce Thought Records

### For Beginners
Start with a simplified 3-column version:
1. Situation
2. Thought
3. Emotion

Once comfortable, gradually expand to the full 7 columns.

### Therapeutic Prompts
- "I'd like to suggest a simple exercise that many people find helpful for understanding their thoughts better."
- "Would you be open to writing down what went through your mind next time this happens?"
- "Let's walk through this situation together using a structured approach."

## Key Principles
- Thought records are most effective when completed close to the triggering event
- They are a SKILL that improves with practice — don't expect perfection early on
- The therapist should model the process first before asking the person to do it alone
- Normalize difficulty — many people find it hard to identify thoughts at first
- Focus on the process, not the "right" answer

## Common Mistakes
- Writing down interpretations instead of actual thoughts
- Skipping the evidence examination and jumping to "positive thinking"
- Completing records only for major events — small everyday situations work best for practice
- Being self-critical about the thoughts instead of observing them with curiosity
