# Relaxation Techniques

## Overview
Relaxation techniques are fundamental CBT tools used to manage the physiological symptoms of stress and anxiety. These techniques activate the parasympathetic nervous system, counteracting the fight-or-flight response. Regular practice builds resilience and gives individuals practical tools they can use anywhere.

## When to Use
- During acute anxiety or panic
- Before sleep to manage insomnia
- As a daily stress management practice
- Before exposure exercises
- When physical tension is high (headaches, muscle tightness, stomach issues)
- When someone feels overwhelmed but can't identify specific thoughts

## Technique 1: Diaphragmatic (Deep) Breathing

### The 4-7-8 Method
1. Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds
2. Hold your breath for 7 seconds
3. Exhale slowly through your mouth for 8 seconds
4. Repeat 4-6 times

### Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)
1. Inhale for 4 seconds
2. Hold for 4 seconds
3. Exhale for 4 seconds
4. Hold empty lungs for 4 seconds
5. Repeat 4-6 times

### Therapeutic Guidance
"Let's try something together right now. Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly. As you breathe in slowly, try to make the hand on your belly rise while keeping the chest hand still. This means you're breathing deeply into your diaphragm."

## Technique 2: Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR)

### Process
Work through major muscle groups, tensing each for 5-7 seconds, then relaxing for 15-20 seconds.

Order:
1. Hands and forearms — make a tight fist
2. Upper arms — bend at the elbow, flex biceps
3. Forehead — raise eyebrows high
4. Face — squeeze eyes shut, scrunch nose
5. Jaw — clench teeth gently
6. Neck and shoulders — shrug shoulders toward ears
7. Chest — take a deep breath and hold
8. Stomach — tighten abdominal muscles
9. Thighs — press legs together firmly
10. Calves and feet — point toes forward, then pull toward shins

### Therapeutic Guidance
"Notice the contrast between the tension and the relaxation. That sensation of letting go — that's what we're building. With practice, you can learn to release tension without needing to tense first."

## Technique 3: Body Scan Meditation

### Process
1. Find a comfortable position, close your eyes
2. Starting from the top of your head, slowly move your attention down through your body
3. At each area, simply notice any sensations without trying to change them
4. Spend 20-30 seconds on each body part
5. When you reach your toes, take three deep breaths and slowly open your eyes

### Therapeutic Guidance
"This isn't about changing anything — it's about noticing. Many of us carry tension we're not even aware of. The body scan helps you tune back into your body."

## Technique 4: Guided Imagery

### Process
1. Close your eyes and imagine a place where you feel completely safe and at peace
2. Engage all five senses: What do you see? Hear? Smell? Feel? Taste?
3. Stay in this place for 3-5 minutes
4. When ready, slowly return your attention to the room

### Therapeutic Guidance
"Your safe place can be real or imaginary. It could be a beach, a forest, a cozy room, or even floating in space. There's no wrong answer — it just needs to feel peaceful to you."

## Key Principles
- Relaxation is a SKILL — it gets easier and more effective with regular practice
- Some techniques work better for some people than others — experimentation is encouraged
- Start with the easiest technique and build from there
- It's normal for the mind to wander during practice — this doesn't mean it's not working
- Pair relaxation practice with a specific time of day to build a habit
- If a technique increases anxiety (rare but possible), stop and try a different one
