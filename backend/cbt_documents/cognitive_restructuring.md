# Cognitive Restructuring

## Overview
Cognitive restructuring is a core CBT technique that helps individuals identify, challenge, and modify unhelpful thought patterns. The goal is to replace distorted or irrational thoughts with more balanced, accurate ones.

## When to Use
- When a person is experiencing negative automatic thoughts
- When someone is stuck in a pattern of catastrophizing, overgeneralizing, or black-and-white thinking
- When emotional distress is driven primarily by how a situation is interpreted, not the situation itself

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify the Situation
Ask the person to describe the specific situation that triggered their distress. Keep it factual and concrete.

Example prompt: "Can you tell me what happened right before you started feeling this way?"

### Step 2: Identify Automatic Thoughts
Help the person recognize the thoughts that came to mind automatically during the situation. These are often quick, unquestioned, and feel absolutely true.

Example prompt: "When that happened, what was the first thought that went through your mind?"

### Step 3: Identify the Emotion and Intensity
Ask the person to name the emotion they felt and rate its intensity from 0-100.

Example prompt: "What emotion did that thought bring up? On a scale of 0 to 100, how intense was that feeling?"

### Step 4: Examine the Evidence
Guide the person to look at evidence both for and against their automatic thought.

Questions to ask:
- "What evidence supports this thought?"
- "What evidence goes against it?"
- "Have there been times when this thought wasn't true?"
- "What would you tell a close friend who had this thought?"

### Step 5: Generate an Alternative Thought
Help the person develop a more balanced, realistic thought.

Example prompt: "Based on all the evidence, is there a more balanced way to look at this situation?"

### Step 6: Re-rate the Emotion
After generating the alternative thought, ask the person to re-rate their emotional intensity.

## Key Principles
- Cognitive restructuring is NOT about positive thinking — it's about accurate thinking
- Validate the person's emotions before challenging their thoughts
- Use Socratic questioning rather than telling someone their thought is wrong
- This technique works best when practiced regularly, not just during crises
- The goal is to build a habit of noticing and questioning automatic thoughts

## Common Pitfalls
- Jumping to restructuring before the person feels heard
- Forcing a positive thought instead of finding a genuinely balanced one
- Using this technique when the person is in acute crisis (stabilize first)
