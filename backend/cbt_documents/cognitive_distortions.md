# Cognitive Distortions

## Overview
Cognitive distortions are systematic patterns of biased thinking that lead to inaccurate perceptions of reality. They are automatic, habitual, and often feel completely true to the person experiencing them. Identifying these patterns is the first step in cognitive restructuring.

## The 15 Common Cognitive Distortions

### 1. All-or-Nothing Thinking (Black-and-White Thinking)
Seeing things in absolute categories with no middle ground.
- Example: "If I don't get an A, I'm a complete failure."
- Challenge: "Is there a middle ground between perfect success and total failure?"

### 2. Overgeneralization
Drawing broad conclusions from a single event.
- Example: "I got rejected for one job. I'll never get hired anywhere."
- Challenge: "Does one experience really predict all future experiences?"

### 3. Mental Filter
Focusing exclusively on negative details while ignoring positive aspects.
- Example: Getting 10 positive comments and 1 negative one, then dwelling only on the negative.
- Challenge: "What are you filtering out? What else happened?"

### 4. Disqualifying the Positive
Dismissing positive experiences by insisting they "don't count."
- Example: "They only complimented me because they felt sorry for me."
- Challenge: "What makes you so sure there's a hidden motive?"

### 5. Jumping to Conclusions
Making negative interpretations without evidence. Two subtypes:
- **Mind Reading**: Assuming you know what others think. "She thinks I'm boring."
- **Fortune Telling**: Predicting negative outcomes. "This will definitely go wrong."
- Challenge: "Do you have evidence for this, or are you guessing?"

### 6. Magnification and Minimization
Exaggerating the importance of negatives and shrinking positives.
- Example: "Making that small mistake was catastrophic" while also "That award was no big deal."
- Challenge: "How would someone else see this situation?"

### 7. Catastrophizing
Expecting the worst-case scenario to happen.
- Example: "If I fail this exam, my life is over."
- Challenge: "What's the most likely outcome? What would you do if the worst did happen?"

### 8. Emotional Reasoning
Believing that feelings are facts.
- Example: "I feel like a failure, so I must be one."
- Challenge: "Just because you feel something, does that make it true?"

### 9. Should Statements
Using "should," "must," or "ought to" to set rigid expectations.
- Example: "I should be further along in my career by now."
- Challenge: "Says who? Where does this rule come from?"

### 10. Labeling
Attaching a fixed, global label to yourself or others.
- Example: "I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake."
- Challenge: "Is this a label or a description of one moment?"

### 11. Personalization
Blaming yourself for events outside your control.
- Example: "My friend is in a bad mood — I must have done something wrong."
- Challenge: "Could there be other reasons for their mood?"

### 12. Blame
The opposite of personalization — holding others entirely responsible for your distress.
- Example: "It's entirely my partner's fault that I'm unhappy."
- Challenge: "What part of this situation is within your control?"

### 13. Fallacy of Change
Expecting others to change to suit your needs.
- Example: "If only they would change, I'd be happy."
- Challenge: "What can you change about your own response?"

### 14. Always Being Right
Needing to prove your opinions are correct at the expense of others' feelings.
- Example: Going to great lengths to demonstrate you were right in an argument.
- Challenge: "Is being right more important than this relationship?"

### 15. Fallacy of Fairness
Feeling resentful because the world isn't operating by your rules of fairness.
- Example: "It's not fair that they got promoted and I didn't."
- Challenge: "Life often isn't fair — how can you respond constructively?"

## How to Use This in Conversation
When a person expresses a distorted thought:
1. Validate the emotion first: "I can see why you'd feel that way."
2. Gently name the pattern: "I notice that thought might be an example of all-or-nothing thinking."
3. Explore it together: "What might a more balanced perspective look like?"
4. Never use distortion labels as weapons or to dismiss someone's experience.

## Key Principle
Everyone uses cognitive distortions — they're part of being human. The goal isn't to eliminate them but to recognize them and choose whether to believe them.
